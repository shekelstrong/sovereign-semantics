38 million rubles for one deepfake call. July 2025, Sverdlovsk Oblast: fraudsters synthesized the governor's voice and redirected budget funds to a shell account within 15 minutes of conversation. Technology outran organizational barriers faster than anyone could build them. The question is not whether AI works. The question is who controls the algorithm, whose data trained it, and who reads the logs.

By 2026, 67 of 89 Russian regions integrate AI tools into governance. The Ministry of Digital Development reports citizen appeal processing time dropped from 3.4 to 0.2 days. The Accounts Chamber recovered 1.2 billion ₽ through LLM analysis of government contracts. Simultaneously, FSB and FSTEC record that 60-70% of state-owned companies still use Western APIs in critical systems, ignoring recommendations to switch to domestic solutions. Same technology. Transformers, GPUs, fine-tuning. The difference lies in the architecture of meaning — and that architecture determines which way the pendulum swings for the next decade.

## Three models of state AI

Model of control. China integrates facial recognition, predictive policing, and social credit into a unified system. Per Carnegie Endowment's 2024 report, average incident response time in Shenzhen and Hangzhou dropped from 12 to 3 minutes through graph-based predictive policing models. Price tag: total citizen transparency before the state. A person fined for jaywalking watches their social score drop in WeChat Pay. That score affects loan rates, visa processing speed, children's school access. The system delivers. It also dismantles civil society as a functional concept.

Model of non-interference. AI stays in private corporate hands; the state acts as arbiter and regulator. Stanford AI Index 2025: 78% of major US models developed by private companies (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta), 22% from academic and government labs. Regulatory lag ships as a feature, not a bug. FTC fined Rite Aid $4.5M for deploying facial recognition without customer consent — but by the time the fine landed, data on 2 million people had already been collected and sold to third parties. Law trails technology by 3-5 years. Damage during that window becomes irreversible.

Model of meaning. AI as service infrastructure: fast government services, predictive medicine, logistics optimization without social scoring. Own models (YandexGPT, GigaChat, Saiga), own data, own jurisdiction. Precedents exist. Estonia with X-Road and e-Estonia. UAE with Mohamed bin Zayed University of AI. South Korea with a "digital citizen twin" stripped of scoring components. One condition: political will to resist sliding into the control model.

The problem runs deeper. Meaning requires elite maturity that technology cannot manufacture. Control comes packaged and ready: China sells "Safe City" systems starting at $50M per installation. Non-interference imports by default: using OpenAI in government locks dependency on a foreign vendor. Meaning demands 10-15 years of consistent work across stack, regulation, and deployment culture. Interruption at any stage throws the country back to one of the first two models. Related material: [[sovereign-tech]]

## Where AI already works

Citizen appeal processing. LLM classifier routes complaints to departments in seconds. In the Ministry of Digital Development's pilot covering 1.2 million appeals, average routing time collapsed from 3.4 to 0.2 days. Misrouting share fell from 18% to 6%. The model trains on historical data and cross-checks results against a directory — it does not invent departments.

Predictive medicine. Sverdlovsk Oblast, 2025: a GigaChat-based pilot analyzes 47 parameters from electronic medical records and generates follow-up examination recommendations for physicians. Over 8 months, 2,300 cases of early-stage type 2 diabetes were caught. Standard screening would have found them 2-3 years later.

Transport and logistics. Moscow's "smart traffic light" system since 2023 reduced average rush-hour travel time by 12-18% (Moscow Transport Department, 2024). The algorithm factors in pedestrian flow, weather, metro schedules, and Luzhniki stadium event calendars. Next step: car-sharing and taxi integration for demand balancing.

Budget planning. The Accounts Chamber runs an LLM assistant analyzing government contracts worth 1.8 trillion ₽ annually. 2025 report: 412 procurements flagged for price inflation markers of 7-23%, recovering 1.2 billion ₽ for the budget.

Law enforcement. Arbitration courts deployed an LLM judge's assistant in 2025. Relevant precedents surface in 5 seconds instead of 30 minutes of manual search. Risk: the algorithm can mold decisions to past practice, entrenching the status quo. In assistant mode rather than arbiter mode, the risk stays manageable.

Emergency response. The Federal Forestry Agency's "Atlas" system in 2025 cut average forest fire detection time from 4.2 to 1.1 hours by analyzing Sentinel-2 satellite imagery every 15 minutes paired with weather data.

## Failure points

Citizen scoring. Formally convenient: "smart" appeal routing, priority for those who "need it more." De facto discrimination by proxy attributes. The algorithm determines that residents of a specific district carry higher probability of overdue payments — and delays their social assistance approvals. This discrimination stays invisible and nearly impossible to prove. No human made the decision; an "objective model" did. No court in the Russian Federation ruled against state AI for discrimination in 2024-2025. No procedure exists for "appealing an algorithm."

Biometrics without alternatives. When a service cannot be accessed without biometrics, that amounts to coercion, not sovereignty. Per Ministry of Digital Development data, 9.4 million Russians submitted biometrics in 2025. In Tatarstan and Moscow, biometrics became de facto mandatory for accessing new categories of electronic government services. Same trap as the control model. Different face.

Dependence on foreign models. Running OpenAI for critical government services transfers infrastructure to a foreign jurisdiction. In March 2025, FSB and FSTEC issued recommendations essentially mandating state-owned companies to abandon OpenAI and Google AI in critical systems by 2027. Practical compliance sits at 60-70%. The rest keeps running on Western APIs.

Deepfake attacks on officials. 14 recorded cases in 2025 of deepfakes impersonating governors and ministers in video calls with subordinates. Sverdlovsk Oblast, July 2025: 38 million ₽ transferred to fraudsters via a fabricated "governor's" directive. Defense requires mandatory second-channel verification for critical commands. This remains an organizational problem, not a technological one.

## What to do

- Invest in domestic LLMs. YandexGPT, GigaChat, Saiga need scaling and open-sourcing where safe. The 2024-2026 budget for these projects: 28 billion ₽, or 0.04% of federal expenditure. US government AI spending in fiscal 2025: $4.6 billion (~430 billion ₽), excluding defense programs. Russia spends 15 times less, and model quality reflects it. See also: [[ai-agent-architecture-2026]]

- Build a hard regulatory fence around biometrics and scoring. Through law, not "recommendations." Federal Law 572-FZ "On Biometrics" (2024) regulates storage but not algorithmic use in decision-making. A second part of the law must directly prohibit automatic denial of government services based on AI assessment and mandate human review for all critical decisions. Without this fence, biometrics and scoring expand de facto.

- Open data where security permits. Open government datasets in Russia comprise roughly 12% of total volume, compared to 38% in the US and 31% in the EU. McKinsey Global Institute estimates open data contributes 0.5-1.5% of GDP through reduced transaction costs. For Russia, that translates to 700-2,100 billion ₽ annually. Business and academia can build atop this data.

- Decentralize AI procurement. Regional mini-stacks instead of a single federal monopoly. A unified federal stack creates a single point of failure and political dependency. Regional mini-stacks (GigaChat for Tatarstan, YandexGPT for Moscow, local models for regions with limited connectivity) yield resilience and competition. Higher costs. Exponentially lower cost of failure.

## What it actually means

Russia needs a model of meaning. This is an engineering choice, not an ideological one. Control delivers efficiency at the cost of freedom. Non-interference delivers freedom at the cost of sovereignty. Meaning demands investment, regulatory maturity, and political will — but delivers both.

By 2027, regions that failed to build their own AI stack will face a binary choice: a packaged Chinese "Safe City" installation or Western APIs with built-in vendor intelligence access. The third path requires money, talent, and political will right now. In 18 months, the "build your own" option will cost an order of magnitude more. Architecture of meaning gets built deliberately, or it does not get built at all.

### Q: Can Model 3 be built on open-source LLMs like Llama-4 or Qwen3?

Yes, and this works for regions with 50-100 million ₽ per year for AI. A 2×A100 or H100 cluster plus fine-tuning on domain data (legislation, registries, appeals) produces a model competitive with YandexGPT or GigaChat on narrow tasks. Limitation: context window size and quality on general conversational tasks. For the "sovereign" segment (defense, intelligence, critical infrastructure), this is an advantage — no data leakage to the vendor.

### Q: Which countries sit closest to Model 3?

Singapore (Smart Nation without scoring), Estonia (X-Road and e-Residency), South Korea (digital citizen twin with opt-out right), partially UAE (federal AI stack built on G42). All four spend 0.4-0.7% of budget on AI in governance. All have domestic models (Sea-Lion in Singapore, Kratt in Estonia, HyperCLOVA X in Korea, Falcon and Jais in UAE). All maintain regulatory fences around biometrics and automated decisions. Functioning systems worth benchmarking against.

### Q: How does Model 3 differ from European e-Government?

European e-Government builds services atop Western LLMs (Mistral, OpenAI, Gemini) with emphasis on GDPR and transparency. Model 3 uses its own stack (YandexGPT, GigaChat, Saiga), stores data in national jurisdiction, and rejects social scoring. Formally similar. Fundamentally opposed: e-Government delegates ethics to the Western vendor. Model 3 builds ethics domestically.

### Q: Won't Russian AI turn into scoring under a different name?

Already happening in select regions. In 2025, Moscow, Tatarstan, and Bashkortostan test a "digital citizen profile" — formally for social service matching, de facto edging toward scoring. Model 3 requires a hard regulatory fence: the law must ban automatic service denial based on AI assessment, mandate human review, and guarantee appeal rights. The fence does not exist yet. Scoring expands de facto.

### Q: Why not just take open LLMs and skip building your own?

At the start, you can. Many regions do exactly this in 2024-2025. Problems surface at scale: dependence on a foreign vendor for fine-tuning hands them control over the state's "voice"; Western LLM providers must comply with their intelligence services' requests — a non-disableable feature; open-source LLMs at Qwen3-72B or DeepSeek-V3 level demand their own infrastructure ($3,000-6,000/month per cluster) and a team.
